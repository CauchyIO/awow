Coordinating delivery

The forward, operational view of the board — the part of awow that steers on the work rather than feeding, wiring, or reading it.

/project-manager walks the dependency graph the delivery loop has already built: it checks in with the people doing the work and offers to clear their path, keeps the graph correct, reconciles the plan against what is actually happening, and names the few blocks only management can clear. Its --report mode is the weekly roll-up for the management team. Reporting is the by-product, not the job.

standardise command needs a graph first read-only until a gate

TL;DR

The delivery loop ships stories; this is what coordinates them once more than one person is working shared, dependent work. /project-manager loads the delivery graph from the board, checks in with the people behind in-flight and stalled items to unblock them, cross-references the plan against real code and board activity, and re-groups everything into coordination buckets — handoffs ready to fire, blocked/waiting, stale/idling, scarce-resource bottlenecks, and the critical path at risk. It proposes the check-ins, graph corrections, and escalations it could make, and does none of them without explicit approval. Run weekly with --report, the same agent produces the deliberately-simplified management roll-up. It is a project manager, not a project reporter.

Be a project manager, not a project reporter. The difference is action: you check in and offer help, you correct the graph, and you reconcile plan against reality — reporting falls out of that as a by-product. A run that only summarises the board and proposes nothing actionable has missed the brief. The dependency graph is the thing you steer on, and it never tells the whole story, so keeping it correct is part of the work — not a footnote.

Where it sits among its neighbours

Four commands touch the same board from different directions. /project-manager is the only one that looks forward across the whole team to coordinate delivery; the others are personal or retrospective. Reaching for the wrong one is the most common confusion, so the boundary is worth stating plainly.

CommandDirectionWhat it does
/my-workinbound · personalWhat the board is asking of one person — their queue, surfaced and prioritised.
/daily-checkinoutbound · personalSomeone narrates their own day; the agent reconciles it onto the board.
/daily-digest · /weekly-digestretrospective · team-wideWhat the team already shipped, synthesised at rising altitudes.
/project-managerforward · deliveryChecks in across the team to unblock people, keeps the graph true, and surfaces what only management can clear.

What it needs before it can run

This command is bounded on purpose. It does not start cold and it is not a substitute for the work, the design, or the people doing it. It presupposes three things, and the first is the one that matters most.

  1. A delivery graph already exists. Work has been decomposed into dependent tasks — designed by /solution-design-flow, sequenced into a stated dependency graph by /project-plan, and shipped item-by-item through /process-workitem — and is reflected on the board. Where the board carries no formal dependency edges, the command reconstructs them from the solution-design artefacts and the issue content, and marks every inferred edge as inferred.
  2. The agent can read and write the board (Step 0 of /setup-awow complete), through the surface named in context/tooling/board.md.
  3. More than one person or team is coordinating on shared work. For a single person on independent stories, /my-work is the right tool; coordination only earns its keep once handoffs and contended resources exist.
It coordinates a graph — it cannot conjure one. /project-manager surfaces blocks and handoffs and keeps the graph honest. It does not do the work, define the services, or fix adoption — people, the retro loop, and a leadership mandate do that. Run it against an empty or untended board and it has nothing to steer; the answer then is to run the delivery loop first, not to run this harder.

The coordination loop

Two rhythms, like the delivery loop it wraps. The operational loop runs at a daily-to-sprint cadence: load the graph, check in with people, reconcile, compute the coordination read, and stop at a gate — then repeat, without re-nudging work that only just moved. Once a week the same agent, in --report mode, rolls the picture up to the management team.

Board / graph / human — durable Operational loop (daily · sprint) Weekly roll-up (upward) Read / artefact

The graph feeds both rhythms: the operational loop steers on it daily, and the weekly roll-up reads the same graph at a higher altitude. The gate sits between every proposal and any board write — check-ins, graph corrections, and escalations alike.

How a run goes, step by step

A single operational run moves through seven steps. The first four gather and reconcile; the fifth turns that into a decision-shaped read; the last two keep the graph honest and stop at the gate.

StepWhat happens
1 · Resolve scope & cadenceDecide which delivery graph to load — a project, a team, or all active work — and respect cadence. Coordination has a cost; rebalancing too often burns the team the way frequent trades burn returns in fees. Run the operational loop daily-to-sprint, the roll-up weekly, and don’t re-check work that moved hours ago.
2 · Load the delivery graphPull the items in scope and the edges between them — state, assignee, blocked flag, last-update time, parent, acceptance criteria, dependency links. Where edges are missing, infer them from design artefacts and issue content and mark them inferred. Sanity-check thin results before trusting them: an empty graph on work you know is active usually means a mistyped scope or stale credentials.
3 · Check in with the peopleReach out to the owners of in-flight and stalled items rather than only reading their tickets. Keep each check-in to three questions at most — where the work stands, whether anything is blocking, and what would help. The aim is to help and unblock, never to chase. Skip anyone whose work already moved today.
4 · Reconcile plan against actualThe graph is the plan, and it drifts the moment work starts. Cross-reference planned sequence, ownership, estimates, and states against real code activity, board movement, and the check-in answers. Where plan and reality diverge, the plan is usually the stale one — propose realigning it.
5 · Compute the coordination readDon’t dump a status list. Re-group everything into coordination buckets (next section), deciding what each item needs now.
6 · Maintain the dependency graphExpect the graph to be incomplete; keeping it correct is the job. Where it is missing or wrong, propose the fix — a ticket for a component the design named but the board never captured, a real dependency edge, an owner for an unassigned item, acceptance criteria for an item that will stall without them.
7 · Propose and act — at the gateEnd with concrete follow-ups grouped by type, and take none without explicit approval (covered below).
Scope → Load → Check in → Reconcile → Coordination read → Maintain graph → Gate

The coordination read

This is the output that makes the command worth running. Instead of echoing every open issue, the work is re-grouped by what each item needs — so the read is a set of decisions, not a status board. The critical path is surfaced first.

surface first

Critical path at risk

Items on the critical path that are slow or blocked threaten the whole delivery, not just their own branch. They lead the read.

momentum

Handoffs ready to fire

A predecessor just completed and its dependents are now unblocked. Name the finished item, the newly-ready successor, and its owner — these move the moment someone is told.

waiting

Blocked / waiting

Name the single person or dependency each item waits on, and split team-resolvable (the team can clear it) from escalation-only (only management can).

flow signal

Stale / idling

In progress with no movement in N working days (default 3), or people idling because an upstream dependency is stuck. Idling is a flow signal about the graph — not a judgement about people.

Scarce-resource bottlenecks are the escalation candidate. When several items queue behind one contended resource — a single security reviewer, a shared environment, one architect — the teams cannot solve it themselves. This is exactly what management exists to decide: who gets the resource, or whether it can be replicated. (It is the contended-resource case the resource-steering picture is built around.)

The gate — propose, never act silently

Everything up to this point is read-only. The command ends by showing concrete follow-ups it could take, grouped by type, and waits. Each is shown so the user can approve it before anything touches a person or the board.

  • Check-ins to send — the per-person messages, shown verbatim so the wording can be approved before any go out.
  • Graph corrections — missing tickets, dependency edges, owners, acceptance criteria, and any board state that drifted from reality — each following the team’s board-output rules, kept to minimum-useful text.
  • Nudges / board actions — a comment recording a blocker, a move for an item whose real state has drifted.
  • Escalations — the items only management can unblock, each stated as the decision needed, not just the problem.
Approve, then execute exactly what was approved. The command asks one question — “Should I send the check-ins, apply the graph corrections and board actions, raise the escalations?” — re-verifies each match before touching it, and if ambiguity surfaces mid-execution, stops and asks. No silent changes, and inferred dependency edges are never written as if confirmed.

The weekly MT roll-up — --report mode

The same agent, reporting upward. Information gets more abstract the higher it goes, so the management team cannot absorb item-level detail — this is deliberately simplified and week-on-week. It is read-only: written to reports/mt/YYYY-Www.md, and never shared without explicit approval. It answers the three things a management team actually decides on.

QuestionWhat the report says — and its honesty rule
1 · On track?Per active project: on track / blocked / slowing. Names only the blockers management can clear — the decisions waiting on them, not the ones teams are handling themselves.
2 · Still delivering the expected value?Whether each project is still worth what it was scoped to deliver. This is the question that surfaces sunk cost — it flags a project whose value has eroded even if it is “on track”.
3 · Has the external signal shifted?Market or external changes that alter the premise a project was started on — a reason to pivot or kill. Grounded in supplied material only: no fabricated market signals, and no external links without approval. Where a scan would help but there is no grounded source, it says so rather than inventing one.
The roll-up answers question 1 today. On-track and management-only blockers read straight off the board. Question 2 leans on a value-measurement instrument the team has to put in place; question 3 needs an outside-in feed the board does not source. The honest report shows what it has and flags what it doesn’t — it does not colour an unknown green.

Anti-patterns — how it stays trustworthy

The command is defined as much by what it refuses to do. These guardrails are why a coordination loop helps rather than becoming overhead or surveillance.

Don’tWhy
Don’t just reportA run that only summarises and proposes nothing actionable has missed the brief. Check in, offer help, correct the graph, reconcile.
Don’t interrogateCheck-ins offer help and clear blockers — never frame them as chasing. The question is “what would help”, not “why isn’t this done”.
Don’t echo the boardA flat list of every open issue is the problem this command solves, not its output. If you’re not re-grouping by coordination action, you’re not done.
Don’t let plan and graph driftWhen code activity and check-ins contradict the board, reconcile. A graph that no longer matches reality is worse than none, because people steer on it.
Don’t over-coordinateRe-running the loop and re-nudging the same people every few hours is overhead that burns the team. Respect cadence; let people work.
Don’t act silentlyRead-only until the gate. Surface drafted check-ins, graph corrections, board actions, and escalations, and wait for the go-ahead.
Don’t invent dependenciesMark inferred edges as inferred; never present a reconstructed dependency as confirmed.
Don’t escalate everythingEscalation is for what the team genuinely cannot resolve — a contended resource, a cross-team priority call. Routine blockers are not management material.
Don’t evaluate peopleIdling and stale items are flow signals about the graph, not judgements about individuals. Check-ins exist to help, not to assess.

Where it plugs into the rest of awow

Coordination is the last link in a chain that all turns on one shared artefact: a stated dependency graph. A design becomes a sequenced plan, the plan becomes board items that know what blocks what, items ship one by one, and delivery is steered against the graph rather than a flat list of tickets.

CommandIts role in the chain
/solution-design-flowTurns a design conversation into a locked design plus a decomposed work-item tree whose children name what blocks them.
/project-planThe bridge — states the dependency graph (nodes, edges, sequence vs parallel, critical path), creates the board items in order with blocked-by links, and publishes a durable plan.
/process-workitemShips each item through the seven-step loop, respecting blocked-by so nothing starts before its dependencies clear.
/project-managerSteers on the published graph — checks in, fires handoffs, reconciles, keeps the graph correct, and rolls up to management.
/solution-design-flow → /project-plan → /process-workitem (× many) → /project-manager
The dependency graph is the connective tissue. It is stated by /project-plan, honoured by /process-workitem, and steered on by /project-manager. Without the graph stated somewhere true, coordination has nothing to read — which is why it is a first-class deliverable, not a by-product of reconciliation.
