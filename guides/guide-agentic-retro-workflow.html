🔁 Retro Workflow ⏱️ ~12 min read

The Agentic Retro Workflow

How a retrospective transcript becomes named anti-patterns, owned actions, and concrete diffs back into your agent's instructions — with two human gates along the way.

The one-liner — point /process-retro at a retro transcript. It analyses what was said, what was implied, and what was conspicuously absent, then proposes tightenings to CLAUDE.md — stopping twice for your sign-off before anything lands.

The shape of it

The retro workflow takes the transcript of a retrospective and turns it into structured signal that closes the loop back into the team's agent instructions — anti-patterns named, actions owned, recurring issues escalated, and concrete diffs proposed for CLAUDE.md / copilot-instructions.md.

The agent works as a sparring partner, not a secretary. It names what was said, what was implied, and what was missing — and every finding comes with reasoning and a verbatim quote, so you can correct it. It runs under one command, /process-retro, as a pipeline with two gates: it stops at each, shows its work, and waits.

The pipeline

Retro transcript
↓
Load context + Prime-Directive check
↓
Parse, classify & analyse → Gate 1 (confirm understanding)
↓
Report + instruction diffs → Gate 2 (approve closures)
↓
Report saved · approved diffs landed in CLAUDE.md

Why it exists

Retros generate good intentions that evaporate. The action items get lost in a doc, or become vague tickets nobody pulls. The agentic twist is sharper still: the artefact isn't the retro notes — it's the instruction-tightening. A recurring problem becomes a concrete diff to the agent's instructions, so the behaviour that caused it is prevented next time. The following retro then verifies whether that diff actually stuck. That is what turns a retro into a learning loop instead of a venting ritual.

What it reads first

Before it touches the transcript, the agent loads whatever team context is available — and proceeds with whatever it finds. It does not gate on context.

  • context/retros/canon.md — the load-bearing grounding: the Prime Directive, the five-phase model, the format taxonomy, and what "good" looks like.
  • context/retros/anti-patterns.md — the maintained library of named issues to probe for.
  • context/team/members.md — names and roles, for speaker attribution and the sentiment pass.
  • context/tooling/board.md — used to verify whether last retro's promises actually landed.
  • retro-reports/<team>/ — prior reports, which unlock trajectory analysis.

It also does a Prime-Directive check: it scans the first few minutes for Norm Kerth's blameless framing — read aloud, paraphrased, or absent — and records which. Absence isn't punishable, but it is observable; the canon treats reading it as the most leveraged twenty seconds in the retro.

1 · Parse, classify & analyse

The agent parses the transcript (WebVTT, plain text, or SRT), re-attributes shared-microphone speech where it can, and then does the analytical heavy lifting:

  • Detects the format the retro was actually run in — open discussion, silent generation, 1-2-4-All, Lean Coffee, TRIZ, futurespective — or flags conversational dominance, which is a pathology, not a chosen format.
  • Filters noise with a discard taxonomy (greetings, demo mechanics, drift). A high discard fraction is itself a signal.
  • Probes for anti-patterns against the library — two sections, kept distinct so you can see what has prior literature behind it and what is agentic-AI-specific and net-new:
venting-ritual action-orphan duplicate-creation attribution-gap prompt-drift board-zombie

Each match carries a verbatim [hh:mm:ss] @Speaker quote. If history exists, it also runs a sentiment & safety pass (talk-time share, cut-offs, hedges, energy) and trajectory analysis — classifying each issue as recurring, new, or resolved.

Gate 1 — confirm understanding. The agent stops and presents a compact summary: detected format, participants, anti-patterns found, recurring issues, sentiment notes, and anything it's unsure about. This is where you correct it — fix an attribution, add a topic it missed — before it drafts the report.

2 · Report & instruction diffs

Once you confirm, the agent composes a structured report. Every claim cites [hh:mm:ss] @Speaker; where there's no signal, it says so rather than padding. The sections group into three bands:

BandWhat's in it
Core
every retro		Header & format · sentiment & safety snapshot · what worked / what didn't · detected anti-patterns (grouped general vs agentic-AI) · action list (severity × scope, every action owned).
Trajectory & closure
when history exists		Cost & velocity · trajectory vs last retro · counter-signal (what was conspicuously absent) · closure tracker against the board.
Loop & audiencesInstruction-tightening diffs · three role-conditioned summaries · next-retro format recommendation.
Gate 2 — approve closures. The agent presents the instruction diffs (these change agent behaviour, so review them carefully), the sponsor one-pager that travels upward, and any @unassigned actions. Only what you approve gets written: the report is saved to retro-reports/<team>/, and approved diffs are applied to CLAUDE.md with a provenance comment.

Closing the loop into the instructions

This is the part that makes the workflow agentic rather than just minute-taking. The instruction-tightening section produces actual diffs, not vague suggestions — concrete lines to add to CLAUDE.md, copilot-instructions.md, or a specific prompt, each with the reason it exists:

# Add to CLAUDE.md § board interaction

When creating a board item, first search for an existing one that
covers the scope; link it instead of creating a duplicate.

# Reason: duplicate-creation anti-pattern, 3 instances this retro.

Because the diffs land in version control, the next retro's closure tracker can check whether they actually prevented their target anti-pattern. Recurring nuisances get promoted to blockers automatically — "this is the 3rd retro flagging X" is the highest-value line in the whole report — and issues that have gone quiet for two retros get celebrated as resolved.

Three audiences, one retro

The report ends with three deliberately distinct summaries — not the same content reordered — so the right reader gets the right altitude:

  • Team-local checklist — ~10 lines, tactical, for the team going into next retro.
  • Governance digest — ~½ page of patterns, risks, and trajectory, for a manager-level read.
  • Sponsor one-pager — ~½ page of value, cost, top risks, and the ask, for executive sponsorship.

One transcript, three cadences: the checklist goes into the next standup, the digest goes up monthly, the one-pager quarterly. A solo mode also exists — point it at a voice memo and it skips the sentiment pass and collapses the summaries, but keeps anti-pattern detection on.
