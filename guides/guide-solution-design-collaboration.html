TL;DR

Solution designs are documents that turn an open question into a recorded decision. To work, they need three things this guide addresses: a place (where do they live), a lifecycle (when do they move from sketch to ratified), and a feedback channel (how do other people’s objections land back on the artifact). The last one is the part that’s usually broken — commentary in PRs, Google Docs, and chat all drift away from the design itself, so the trace of "who pushed back and what was decided" is lost.

1. Actors

Five human roles interact with a design, plus an assistive agent. The same person plays different roles at different times; the roles are stable, the headcount per role isn’t.

Author

Writes the design. Owns the question. Responsible for resolving feedback before ratification.

Reviewer

Reads, challenges, comments, approves. Brings perspective the author didn’t have.

Decider

Has final say when reviewers disagree. Often the author, sometimes not.

Implementer

Reads the ratified design and turns it into code / configuration / process. May post-hoc comment when reality bites.

Future reader

Reads it months later trying to understand "why did we do it this way." Trust nobody — this is the role most teams fail.

Agent (assistive)

Drafts, summarises feedback, surfaces inconsistencies. Reads designs and their commentary as context for downstream work.

3. Filing — where designs live

"Where do I put this document?" is one of the easiest questions to leave ambiguous and one of the most expensive to answer badly. The taxonomy below makes it explicit. Five categories, each with a clear purpose; everything else is a mistake.

CategoryWhat goes hereWhat does not
Designs
designs/		 Internal solution designs. Working specs. Documents with a decision in them that the team intends to ratify. Client-facing material. Generated reports. Informal notes.
Decisions
architecture_decision_record/		 Ratified architectural decisions. ADRs, immutable history. In-flight design drafts (those belong in Designs).
Proposals
proposals/		 Client-facing material. Engagement briefs, solution proposals delivered as PDFs. Internal-only thinking. Use Designs.
Notes
notes/		 Meeting notes, informal docs, low-ceremony commits. Anything with a decision the team needs to ratify. Promote to Designs.
Generated
daily_digest/, monthly_digest/, …		 Outputs of automated processes. Read-only from the team’s perspective. Hand-authored material.
The rule: one home per category, no ambiguity. If you have to think about where something goes, the taxonomy needs amending, not the document. Adapt the folder names to whatever convention your repo already uses — the categories are what matters.

4. Lifecycle — sketch → ratified

A design moves through five named states. Each transition has a clear trigger; the trigger answers "when should I commit / push / share / decide."

States of a designbehaviour

StateWhat it means in practiceVisibility
SketchAuthor’s thinking, not yet committed. May live on a branch or uncommitted. No expectation anyone else looks.Local only
DraftPushed to the repo so the author won’t lose it, but not broadcast. Anyone browsing the repo can find it; no one is asked to react.Internal repo, not announced
In reviewAuthor has explicitly invited reviewers. Comments are open. Author actively shepherds the conversation.Announced; rendered on internal site
RatifiedDecider has approved; outstanding feedback either resolved or explicitly accepted as not-blocking. The decision is now binding for downstream work.Internal site, prominent
SupersededA newer design has taken over. Old one is kept for historical context, marked with a pointer forward.Internal site, marked stale
When do I commit? The moment the sketch becomes coherent enough that you yourself would want to refer back to it. Don’t wait for polish — the safety net of commits is the point. Push at the same time. The world doesn’t see it until you advertise it.
When do I share? When you’ve done what you can alone and need other perspectives. State changes from Draft to In review. Posting the link is the moment.
When do I declare it ratified? When the decider says so, in writing, on the design itself (a comment, or a "ratified" marker the author flips). Until then, the design is still being argued with.

5. Feedback loop

Functional view of how a reviewer’s objection lands on the design, gets addressed, and leaves a trace. No technology named — the same flow could be implemented several ways.

Feedback loop — objection to resolutionbehaviour

The two properties this loop must have to beat what most teams do today:

  • The mark stays on the spot. A comment about §5.2 must be readable next to §5.2 forever, not in a chat thread or a closed PR.
  • Resolution preserves the trace. Marking a comment "resolved" hides it but doesn’t delete it. The future reader can expand resolved threads and see the argument.

6. Forking

Two distinct things people call "fork." Treat them differently.

Counter-proposal

Reviewer wants to present a fundamentally different design at the same level of detail. Pattern: a new design document, linked to the original on the work tracker. Both compete for ratification. Discussion happens on each separately, plus a meta-thread comparing them.

Suggested edit

Reviewer wants a sentence to say X instead of Y. Pattern: stays as a comment on the section. Not a fork. The existing feedback loop handles this; spinning up a parallel design for a sentence is overkill.

6.1 When a counter-proposal exists

The original design’s threads are visible on the counter-proposal as read-only context — so the counter-author can see what was already debated. New commentary lands on whichever document the reviewer is reading. The work tracker carries the relationship so neither document feels orphaned.

7. Containers (functional)

One layer below C1 — the “containers” the design lives in, still without naming technologies. Name what each does, not what it’s built with.

Containers — functional rolesC2

7.1 Why this level matters before the next one

If a team picks a technology before agreeing on which functional container does what, the technology drives the shape rather than serving it. The C2 view forces a team to articulate what each container is for; only then does it make sense to ask "which tool plays the commentary-surface role."

For each team to answer

8. Open questions a team should answer

These are the calls each adopting team needs to make. They’re intentionally not decided in this guide — the answers shape how the C2 containers get implemented later.

Q1. Filing taxonomy. Five categories proposed in §3. Are these the right five for your team, or do you need fewer / more? Specifically: is "Designs" worth splitting from "Decisions," or are they the same thing in different lifecycle states?
Q2. Ratification authority. Who decides a design is ratified? Always the author? Always the Decider role? Or does it depend on the kind of design (security vs. product vs. process)?
Q3. Resolved-state semantics. When a comment is resolved, is the resolution itself recorded (who, when, why)? Or is "resolved" a binary flag with no narrative?
Q4. Lifecycle transitions and the work tracker. Should the work tracker reflect the design’s lifecycle state automatically, or is that a separate manual update? The latter is simpler but drifts.
Q5. Scope of commentary — designs only, or every published HTML? Daily digests, proposals to clients, ADRs — which of these get a commentary surface? Decide per category, not per document.
Q6. Future-reader trace bar. The standard suggested here is "three years from now, the future reader can see who pushed back and why." Is that the right horizon for your team? Tighter (one year) might be enough; longer adds storage and search costs.
Q7. Bootstrap pattern. How does a team transition from "ad-hoc PRs and Google Docs" to this model on existing in-flight designs? Lowest-friction interim: keep using PR comments, but copy the conclusions back onto the design’s in-repo comment thread when it’s ratified.
