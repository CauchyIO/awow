The core delivery loop

The loop one person runs, day to day, that proves awow earns its place.

Three commands in two rhythms: through the day you run /refinement-prep and /process-workitem repeatedly — drafting stories and taking work items to PR — and at the end of the day /daily-checkin reconciles what happened onto the board. The first two are the Seed loop; the third is a Standardise companion layered on top.

seed loop + a standardise companion proposal-first throughout

TL;DR

Through the day, two Seed commands run again and again — not once. /refinement-prep turns a brief into a right-sized draft the team reviews ahead of a session; /process-workitem walks a single story from board to PR through a seven-step frame that iterates on a plan, never on production code. Then, at the end of the day, /daily-checkin reconciles what actually happened against the board, biasing hard against noise. Through all three runs the same spine: look at the board first, never duplicate, draft to cheap markdown, land only after a human approves.

One discipline, three commands. The cheap-to-change artefact — a markdown file under proposals/ — is where iteration happens. The board and production code are expensive to change well, so they are touched only at an approval gate. Each command also looks at the board before acting and refuses to create a duplicate. That is the whole way of working in one sentence.

The loop

Two rhythms, not one line. Through the day the Seed commands repeat — /refinement-prep drafts stories and /process-workitem takes them to PR, each running as often as the work demands. At the end of the day /daily-checkin reads that activity and proposes board updates. The check-in caps the day; it feeds the board, not the code.

The three commands at a glance

CommandPhaseWhat it producesThe gate

Why this is the loop that proves the value. No coordination, no ceremony, no second adopter required. One person runs /refinement-prep ahead of sessions and /process-workitem on each story through the day, then closes the day with /daily-checkin — and the team sees the output at the next refinement. The Standardise and Spread commands build on this; they don’t replace it.

/refinement-prep — draft a feature before refinement

The aim is simple: arrive at the refinement session with a draft already in hand, so the meeting becomes design discussion rather than discovery. One person can run it alone; the value shows up in their own work first, and is visible to the whole team at the next session. It is typically the first prompt a team adopts.

What goes in

Any one of: a one-paragraph brief (typed or dropped in input/), a slidedeck or document in input/quarterly/, a board issue URL to expand into stories, or the structured output of /process-transcript.

What it does, in order

  1. Load context. Reads the mission (the feature must serve it — if it can’t see how, it asks before drafting), the REQUIRED conventions (titles, labels, output discipline), the board-output style, the glossary, existing patterns, and the board’s sizing rules.
  2. Check for duplicates — before drafting. It searches the board on keywords from the brief, looking for stories that overlap, a parent feature to attach to, and related work that should shape scope. If anything looks like a duplicate, it stops and reports IDs and titles, then asks whether to proceed, link/extend, or cancel. It will not draft over an unconfirmed duplicate.
  3. Draft to markdown. Output lands at proposals/refinement/<feature-slug>.md — a feature wrapper plus three to seven stories, dependencies, open questions, and risks.
The right-sizing rule. Every story must be shippable by a single session as a working PR. Each one: touches 1–5 files, is describable in 2–3 sentences without hand-waving, leaves no architectural decisions to the agent (the story says what; the codebase says how), and carries 5 or fewer acceptance criteria. Anything that fails these gets split.

Two boundaries worth stating plainly: design decisions the team hasn’t made become open questions, not story bodies; and the command co-authors, it never ghost-writes — it iterates until the feature owner would put their name on the draft in the session. A “kitchen-sink” story (“implement X, add tests, update docs, refactor Y”) is always split.

/process-workitem — take a work item from refinement to PR

Once a story exists on the board, this command carries it to a PR through a generic seven-step frame. The frame is the same for every kind of work; the work-specific rules live in archetype handlers under .agents/commands/_workitem-archetypes/ — feature, bugfix, refactor, doc, and any others a team registers as their work demands.

StepWhat happens
1 · LoadResolve the story from the board (or the local cache) and read it through the user-story template — title, body, tags, acceptance criteria, scope boundary. If the story doesn’t fit the template, stop and repair the story; don’t infer scope.
2 · Classify / routeMatch the story to an archetype handler. If the directory is empty, run generically and suggest scaffolding a handler (captured as a stub under proposals/archetypes/) — don’t block. If handlers exist but none match, the story is too broad (split it) or needs a new handler (ask).
3 · Validate inputsRun the archetype’s checks before any planning. Working from assumed state is the most common cause of agent-driven bugs. If anything blocks, stop.
4 · PlanDraft a plan in proposals/<work-item-id>.md — story anchor, file-by-file changes, risks, verification. Get approval before touching code. Iterate here, in the cheap artefact.
5 · ApplyExecute the approved plan without drifting. If scope needs to grow, raise it and amend the plan first. Respect output-placement rules — a story can’t absorb knowledge-base content.
6 · VerifyRun the archetype’s checks: tests / build / lint pass, each acceptance criterion has evidence, behaviour changes get a smoke check. If anything is red, stop and report.
7 · ReportOpen the PR linked to the work item, summarise the changes and verification, advance the board state, and surface any manual follow-ups.
Load → Classify/route → Validate → Plan → Apply → Verify → Report

The principles that hold it together

Iterate on the plan

The plan is cheap to change; the codebase is not. All the back-and-forth happens in proposals/<id>.md before a line of production code is written.

Validate before acting

Never act on un-validated assumptions about state. Check inputs first; check in with the user before each irreversible step.

Stay in scope

The story defines the boundary. Related work becomes separate proposals. Don’t gold-plate — observability, refactors, and docs are follow-up stories.

/daily-checkin — the daily companion

phase: standardise Unlike the two Seed commands, this one belongs to the Standardise phase — a daily companion layered on top of the Seed loop, not part of it. It captures an individual’s working day, maps it onto the board, cross-references it against code activity, and proposes updates.

It is optional and elastic. A detailed account yields a rich summary; a couple of lines yields a lighter one; nothing at all still works, because the board and code activity carry most of the signal. It is not performance evaluation and not forecasting — it enforces structural clarity, making sure the work that happened is reflected on the board and not silently missing a ticket.

Its whole point is biasing against noise. Run every day, a check-in is a noise machine if you let it be — duplicate tickets, verbose recaps, a new issue for every passing thought. The default is the opposite: advance work that is already tracked (comment on or move an existing issue), and treat creating a new issue as the exception that must justify itself. If the proposed list has more new issues than updates, it’s almost certainly generating noise — re-map.

How it reads the day

  • Up to three input sources, none mandatory: the user’s own account (written note, voice memo, or none), code activity (automatic — commits, PRs, reviews from today), and meeting transcripts (routed through /process-transcript, never parsed inline). When sources conflict, processed transcript beats verifiable code activity beats the user’s account.
  • Written input is taken at face value. Voice-to-text input is treated as unreliable and runs a disambiguation protocol — cross-referencing every proper noun against known people, projects, and repos, and presenting all ambiguous terms for confirmation before producing the summary. No silent guessing.
  • Read-only until the gate. Everything up to board execution is synthesis. No issue is created, moved, or commented on until the user explicitly answers “execute these updates?” — then each match is re-verified before it’s touched.

The output is deliberately short: a 2–4 sentence summary, one line per concrete thing that moved (tied to its board issue and commit/PR on the same line), a clearly-marked not yet executed list of proposed updates, and genuine gaps only — “no gaps today” is a fine and common outcome.

The spine that runs through all three

These commands share a small set of rules that live in .agents/CLAUDE.md and the team’s conventions. They are not optional flavour — they are why the loop stays trustworthy as it repeats.

RuleWhat it means in the loop
Board look-firstBefore starting anything with a discernible outcome, search the board for an existing ticket. /refinement-prep does it before drafting; /daily-checkin does it before proposing; /process-workitem starts from a ticket that already exists.
Never duplicateIf a ticket already covers the scope, use it — don’t create a second. Duplicate detection is an explicit, blocking step in /refinement-prep, and the anti-reflex at the heart of /daily-checkin.
Proposal-firstAlways draft to a markdown file under proposals/ first; the board, the knowledge base, and production code are landed only after a human approves. Iterate on the cheap artefact, never on the expensive one.
Output disciplineMinimum-useful text; intent → story body, status → comment, durable rationale → knowledge base. A story is never allowed to absorb content that belongs elsewhere.
The cheap artefact is the iteration surface. A draft feature, a work-item plan, a daily summary — all are markdown under proposals/. That is where the conversation happens. The board and the codebase change once, at the end, after approval. Get that habit right and the rest of awow is built on solid ground.
