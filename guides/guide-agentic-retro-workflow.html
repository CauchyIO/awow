Guide / Agentic Retrospective Workflow
Welcome

From Retro to Action Loop

This guide walks you through the agentic retrospective workflow — a method for turning a 15–20 minute team retro into structured action items, anti-pattern detection, governance signal, and concrete tightenings of your team's agent instructions.

Retro meeting ─→ VTT transcript ─→ /process-retro ─→ Retro report ├─→ Team checklist ├─→ Governance digest ├─→ Sponsor one-pager └─→ Instruction diffs ─┐ ▼ CLAUDE.md copilot-instructions.md

The philosophy: the retro is not the artefact — the instruction-tightening is. Each week's retro produces concrete diffs that close the loop back into how the agent works, and the next retro verifies whether those diffs actually prevented the issues they targeted.

Works with any AWOW team adopting agentic workflows. You need a way to record the retro (Teams, Zoom, etc.), an agent surface (GitHub Copilot in Agent mode or Claude Code), and a repository with the prompt installed.

Background

The Retrospective Canon

The agentic retro is not a new ceremony. It sits inside two decades of practice that started with Toyota's hansei, was named by Norm Kerth in 2001, and was codified by Esther Derby & Diana Larsen in 2006. Everything /process-retro does maps onto that canon — it just automates the parts a meeting tool can do better than a facilitator with a flip-chart.

The five-phase model

Derby & Larsen's Agile Retrospectives↗ separates a retro into five phases. Most pathological retros conflate them; the discipline is keeping them distinct.

1
Set the Stage
Purpose + Prime Directive. Done by the team.
2
Gather Data
/process-retro sections 1–5.
3
Generate Insights
/process-retro sections 8–9.
4
Decide What to Do
/process-retro sections 6 & 11.
5
Close
Team appreciation + meta-retro.

The agent handles the middle three phases. Phases 1 and 5 are irreducibly human — the team frames the session and closes it.

The Prime Directive

Norm Kerth's opening framing↗ is the closest thing the field has to a constitutional clause. Read it aloud at the top of every retro. It binds the team to a blameless frame before anything is revealed.

"Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand." — Norm Kerth, Project Retrospectives (2001)

If you do nothing else, do this. Skip the templates, skip the formats — but read the Prime Directive at the top of every retro and the rest is recoverable.

Step 1

Set Up Your Repository

Required

Before your first retro, your repository needs:

  1. The prompt — process-retro.prompt.md in .github/prompts/ (Copilot) or .claude/commands/ (Claude Code). Copy from the AWOW starter pack.
  2. A transcripts folder — .github/transcripts/ (or equivalent) where you drop the .vtt files. Add it to .gitignore; transcripts are working files and shouldn't be committed.
  3. A retro-reports folder — retro-reports/<team>/. The prompt writes its outputs here. This is committed — it's your team's trajectory history.
# .gitignore
.github/transcripts/

# but keep
!retro-reports/

Optional but recommended. Add a context/retros/anti-patterns.md file. It's the catalogue of named issues the prompt will actively probe for. Ships with ~10 starter patterns; add your own as you discover them.

Step 2

Pick Your Retro Format

Required

There is not one right way to run a retro. Format choice depends on team maturity, who's loud, and whether the previous format produced honest signal. Pick deliberately — the prompt will detect what you used and recommend the next one. Most of these patterns have established names in the literature; we use them where they exist.

Open discussion
Continuous conversation, no silent phase. Often paired with a sticky-note template (Mad/Sad/Glad, Plus/Delta) for structure. Best for young teams that need to vent before they can think.
Silent generation + walk-through
Solo writing on cards, then a structured walk-through. Templates: Starfish↗, 4Ls↗, KALM, DAKI. Use when one voice is dominating and quieter members aren't getting in.
1-2-4-All · silent → pair → group
Solo (1 min) → pairs (2 min) → fours (4 min) → all. From Liberating Structures↗. The structured form of what most teams informally call "silent stickies then discussion." Strong default.
Rotating activities
Cycle through templates (Sailboat, Timeline, TRIZ, Futurespective) on purpose. Derby & Larsen support varying when the signal goes stale — not for novelty's sake.
Consistent template
Same template every retro. Predictability is its own value for teams that need it; the canon supports this as long as the signal still arrives. Vary only when it stops doing so.
Conversational dominance · pathology
Not a choice — what emerged when one speaker held the floor disproportionately. Named in Google's Project Aristotle↗ as a negative predictor. The prompt flags this; next retro switches format.

Cadence. Weekly, 15–20 minutes, low ceremony. The retro is a heartbeat, not an event. Long retros are usually a sign you're catching up on weeks of suppressed signal — fix the cadence first.

On personality-based facilitation. Folk wisdom about "introvert-friendly" or "analytical team" retro styles is widespread but weakly supported. What the literature does back is that written-first-then-spoken structures (1-2-4-All, silent generation) raise participation in every team — regardless of personality typing. Use the structures, skip the labels.

Step 3

Run the Retro & Record It

Required

Open the retro in your meeting tool with recording and transcription enabled. The transcript is the artefact the prompt needs — not the recording itself.

  1. Start the meeting with transcription explicitly on (Teams shows it in the meeting controls; Zoom needs it enabled in account settings).
  2. Read the Prime Directive aloud. Yes, every time. It is the single most leveraged 20 seconds in the entire retro. The agent will pick up the framing in the transcript and treat the conversation accordingly.
  3. Run the format you picked. If 1-2-4-All: open with one minute of silent writing on what worked, what didn't, and what needs attention.
  4. Keep to time-box. 15–20 minutes total. If you overrun consistently, your retros are doing too many jobs — split governance topics into a separate session.
  5. Close with explicit action ownership. Every action gets a name attached, even tentatively. Anonymous actions don't ship.

Psychological safety matters more than format. Amy Edmondson's research↗ identifies team psychological safety as the strongest predictor of team learning. The prompt will detect hedges, cut-offs, and conversational dominance. If a retro consistently produces flat affect or one-voice transcripts, the format isn't the problem — the safety floor is.

Step 4

Download the Transcript

Required

After the retro:

  1. Open the meeting in your tool (Teams, Zoom, etc.).
  2. Locate the transcript and download as .vtt (WebVTT format).
  3. Save into your .github/transcripts/ folder with a date-and-team filename.
.github/transcripts/2026-05-22-platform-team-retro.vtt

VTT preserves speaker tags and timestamps, which the prompt uses for citation and turn-taking analysis. Don't use plain text exports — you'll lose attribution.

If your tool only exports plain text, you can still run the prompt, but several sections (sentiment snapshot, dominance detection, citations) will be degraded.

Step 5

Tell the Agent What You Want

Required

Open a new agent session — clean context produces better analysis. Then, and this is the part that surprises people, just describe what you want in your own words. Attach the VTT file if you have it.

Process this retro for the platform team. It's the one from yesterday in .github/transcripts/. Compare it against the last few we've run so we can see if anything keeps coming back.
Here's the VTT from our retro this morning (file attached). Have a look and tell me what you find. The board is in context/tooling/board.md if you want to check what we promised last time.

You don't need to learn flags or remember argument syntax. The agent reads what you wrote, looks at the files you pointed to (or finds them itself), and figures out:

  • Which transcript to analyse — from your attachment, your path, or by looking in the obvious place.
  • Which team this is for — from the filename, your prose, or context/team/members.md.
  • Whether to do trajectory analysis — if you mention earlier retros, or if retro-reports/<team>/ already exists.
  • Whether to check closure — if you mention "what we promised" or "did we fix", or if a board file is reachable.

The shorthand also works. If you prefer it: /process-retro path/to/retro.vtt. But you're not expected to memorise an interface — the agent meets you where you are.

Step 6

Watch It Work — Two Gates

Required

The prompt runs as a pipeline with two gates. The agent stops at each gate, shows you its work, and waits.

Gate 1 · Confirm understanding

The agent presents a short summary: detected format, participants, anti-patterns it found, recurring issues vs. last retros, sentiment notes, and anything it's unsure about. This is where you correct it before the heavy lifting.

Looks right, but Pat wasn't actually the one who raised the duplicate-creation issue — that was Sam. And we did discuss the security topic, briefly, around the 12-minute mark.

Gate 2 · Approve closures

The agent presents the proposed instruction-tightening diffs (concrete lines to add to CLAUDE.md or copilot-instructions.md) and the sponsor one-pager before it goes anywhere. You approve, redact, or reject.

Land the duplicate-check diff. Hold the attribution-marker one — we tried something similar last month and it didn't stick. Redact the cost number from the sponsor pager; I'll add it manually.

You're always in the loop. The agent never edits CLAUDE.md or files board issues without your sign-off at Gate 2. The pipeline is designed to make oversight cheap, not optional.

Step 7

Read the Report

Required

After Gate 2, the agent writes the full report to retro-reports/<team>/<date>-<format>.md. It has 13 sections in fixed order. You don't read it top-to-bottom every time — different sections matter for different readers.

Core · every retro

  • 1 · Header & format — detected format, discard rate, participants
  • 2 · Sentiment & safety snapshot — talk-time share, cut-offs, energy curve
  • 3 · What worked / 4 · What didn't — bulleted with citations
  • 5 · Detected anti-patterns — matched against the library, with quotes
  • 6 · Action list — severity × scope grid, owners, deadlines

Trajectory & closure · when history is available

  • 7 · Cost & velocity — token-spend trajectory if available
  • 8 · Trajectory vs last retro — recurring, new, resolved
  • 9 · Counter-signal — topics that were absent from the discussion
  • 10 · Closure tracker — last retro's promises × current board state

Loop & audiences

  • 11 · Instruction-tightening proposals — concrete CLAUDE.md diffs
  • 12 · Three role-conditioned summaries — team, governance, sponsor
  • 13 · Next-retro format recommendation — based on what was detected

Citation rule. Every output bullet links back to [hh:mm:ss] @Speaker. If a claim has no citation, the agent made it up — flag it back.

Step 8

Three Audiences, One Retro

Required

Section 12 of the report produces three distinct artefacts from the same transcript — not the same content reordered. Each is for a different reader.

12a · Team-local checklist

~10 lines. Tactical. For the team itself, going into next retro.

- [ ] Tighten duplicate-check on board item creation (@alex, next retro)
- [ ] Document the new MCP install steps (@sam, next retro)
- [ ] Decide: do we drop the daily standup or keep it? (@team, week +2)

12b · Governance digest

~½ page. Patterns, risks, trajectory. For manager-level reads. No tactical noise.

12c · Sponsor one-pager

~½ page. Value delivered, cost trajectory, adoption velocity, top 3 risks, ask. For executive sponsorship conversations.

You don't have to share all three every retro. The team-local checklist goes into the next standup; the governance digest goes to your line manager monthly; the sponsor one-pager goes up quarterly. Same source, different cadence.

Step 9

Close the Loop into the Instructions

Required

Section 11 of the report produces actionable diffs — not vague suggestions. These are the lines you add to your agent's instruction surface so the issues the retro surfaced are prevented permanently.

Example diff the agent might propose:

# Add to CLAUDE.md § Linear interaction guidelines

When creating a board item, the agent MUST mark itself as the actor
by appending "— created by agent (session <id>)" to the description.

# Reason: attribution-gap anti-pattern detected this retro, 3 instances.

These diffs land in one of three places:

  • CLAUDE.md — most tightening lives here. Agent-global rules.
  • copilot-instructions.md — the GitHub Copilot mirror for shops on that stack.
  • Specific prompts in .github/prompts/ or .claude/commands/ when the issue is local to one workflow.

Closure verification. The next retro's section 10 (closure tracker) automatically checks whether the diffs you landed actually prevented their target anti-patterns. This is what makes the retro a learning loop, not a venting ritual.

Step 10

Trajectory Across Retros

Optional · Power Move

The real value compounds across retros. If retro-reports/<team>/ already has prior reports, the agent uses them automatically — or you can mention it in prose:

Compare this against the last few retros for the platform team. I want to know if anything keeps coming back.
Retro 1 ─→ report 1 ↓ Retro 2 + history ─→ report 2 (+ recurring/new/resolved deltas) ↓ Retro 3 + history ─→ report 3 (+ "3rd retro flagging duplicates" — escalate) ↓ Retro 4 + history ─→ report 4 (resolved! Issue X gone for 2 retros)

The most valuable governance line in any retro is "this is the 3rd retro flagging X." It promotes a recurring nuisance to a blocker automatically — no human has to remember.

Resolved issues deserve celebration. Section 8 calls out anti-patterns that were flagged in prior retros but are absent this time. Don't skip this — the dopamine of seeing an issue actually go away is what makes teams care about retros.

Step 11

Let the Agent Suggest Next Week's Format

Optional · Recommended

Section 13 of every report recommends the format for the next retro, based on what the agent detected this week. The rule that fired is named so the recommendation is auditable.

  • Dominance detected → next retro: silent stickies or hybrid
  • Same format 3+ retros in a row → next retro: rotate
  • >50 % noise discarded → next retro: tighter agenda or pre-read
  • Blue-team indicators (high consistency, low affect) → keep format, rotate the focus angle
  • None of the above → keep current format, name one small experiment

You can always override the recommendation. The agent is making a default suggestion based on observable signal — you have context it doesn't (an off-site coming up, a difficult team event, a new joiner). Use it as a starting point, not a verdict.

Step 12

Solo Mode — Personal Retros

Optional · Advanced

Not every retro is a team event. Sometimes the most useful retro is a 5-minute voice memo to yourself at the end of a workday. Just say so:

This is a voice memo I recorded for myself, not a team retro. Have a look and tell me what patterns you see.

In solo mode, the agent:

  • Skips the sentiment & safety snapshot — it doesn't make sense for one speaker.
  • Collapses the three audience summaries to just the personal action list.
  • Keeps anti-pattern detection on — solo retros are often where you spot patterns the team would deflect.
  • Still does trajectory analysis against your personal retro history if it exists.

Solo retros are especially useful when adopting an agentic workflow yourself before introducing it to your team. You'll surface the friction your team would otherwise discover the hard way.

Appendix A

The Anti-Pattern Library

Optional · Maintained

A context/retros/anti-patterns.md file is the catalogue the agent actively probes for. It ships with a starter set; you add patterns as your team discovers them.

Note on provenance. General retro anti-patterns are well documented↗ — venting, action-list inflation, hijacked agendas, etc. But the agentic-AI-specific patterns below (duplicate-creation, attribution-gap, ghost-edit, prompt-drift, context-bleed) are net-new. There is no prior canon to draw from; this library is part of what the AWOW community is contributing.

# context/retros/anti-patterns.md

## duplicate-creation
Agent creates a new board item that overlaps an existing one
because the duplicate-check was insufficient or skipped.

## attribution-gap
Work shows under a human's name with no marker that an agent
performed it on the human's behalf.

## ghost-edit
Agent modifies a board item without flagging itself as the actor.

## prompt-drift
Repo instructions no longer match what the agent is actually
doing in practice.

## board-zombie
Items that haven't moved in N retros and no-one owns.

## silent-fail
A task is reported done but no artefact landed in the board
or the work tree.

## acoustic-prioritisation
Loudest-voice prioritisation instead of severity-driven priority.

## context-bleed
Old session context leaks into a new conversation, producing
stale references or wrong file paths.

# (add your own — keep names hyphenated, descriptions ≤3 lines)

Without this file, the agent falls back to an embedded list of ~10 patterns. With it, your team's specific failure modes get named, tracked, and prevented. Treat the library as a living artefact.

Appendix B

What Else the Agent Looks At

Optional · Improves Output

You don't tell the agent about these — it finds them on its own. But if they exist in your repo, the report gets richer:

Team membership

context/team/members.md — names, roles, focus areas. Improves speaker attribution and the sentiment snapshot. Without it, the agent uses whatever names appear in the transcript.

Glossary

context/knowledge-base/glossary.md — domain terms and abbreviations. Helps disambiguate transcription errors and gives the agent vocabulary for the discard taxonomy.

Board pointer

context/tooling/board.md — which board this team uses and how it's reached. Unlocks the closure tracker (section 10) — the agent can verify whether last retro's promises actually landed.

Prior retro reports

retro-reports/<team>/ — committed history of past reports. Unlocks trajectory analysis (section 8) and lets the agent escalate recurring issues.

Activity log

A reachable agent-activity / token-spend log — unlocks cost & velocity (section 7). The agent doesn't fabricate this; if it can't find a log, the section is omitted cleanly.

You can also just mention them in prose if they live somewhere unusual: "the spend log is in logs/agents/may.jsonl". The agent reads what you wrote and looks where you pointed.
