Why awow exists

Above a certain size, every organisation develops a fog: what is actually happening right now? gets three or four answers depending on who you ask. awow clears it by making the board the single source of truth and giving an agent the context to keep it honest.

What a week looks like

One feature — SSO for the customer portal — from a design call to a published roll-up. Different flows, run by whoever needs them, none waiting on the others — every beat lands on the same board.

MON

/process-transcript routes it; /solution-design-flow captures the C2 view.
Board: epic + 3 stories created
TUE
Refinement
/refinement-prep drafts the stories; the team refines them live.
Board: 3 stories → Ready
WED
Build
/process-workitem takes “Login with SSO” from refinement to a PR.
Board: story → In Review, linked to PR #142
THU

Board: progress logged; one story flagged blocked

Board: blocker owned; trajectory published

Five flows, one feature, one board. No flow waited on another — they just kept meeting at the board, and by Friday it told the whole story on its own.

guide-delivery-coordination.htmlOpen guide →
It coordinates a graph — it can’t conjure one. /project-manager presupposes a delivery graph already on the board. It surfaces blocks and keeps the graph honest — it doesn’t do the work or define the services. Run it on an empty board and it has nothing to steer.

03

Pillar · cross-teamcenter of gravity · the service RACI

One tier above the team. A pillar has no board — it has the service map that says which team owns what, and governs the boundaries between teams. A different archetype: it publishes contracts down and aggregates signal up, where a team repo only consumes.

▲

This leaves the team’s board behind. The center of gravity becomes the activity×team RACI, the rhythm is event-triggered rather than daily, and the work is governing boundaries — not shipping stories. Proposed, not yet built.

The thinking behind it

Where these guides come from

The guides are the hands-on layer. The reasoning underneath — the coordination fog, the board-as-graph reframe, ceremonies rethought, governance, and a worked rollout — lives in the published Agentic Way of Working methodology and its case study. The guides distil what you need; the full argument is the long read.

Scopes, not steps. There’s no sequence to march through and no badge for finishing. Most teams live at the team tier and lean on the delivery loop first; the individual tier sharpens the people doing the work, and the pillar tier appears only once several teams need their boundaries governed together. Each runs on its own clock.
